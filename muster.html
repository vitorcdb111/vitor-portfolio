Muster Place

Released: 2025

Studio: Oak3 Game Studio / Evoluto Solutions

Platforms: WebGL

Skill Focus: Game Design, Leadership, Production Management

Time on Project: ~ 18 months

Team size: 10 people

Engine: Unity

Link: https://muster.place/

WHAT IS THE PROJECT?

Muster is a gamified remote work platform designed to improve collaboration, presence, and team engagement through a shared 3D virtual office, customizable avatars, and productivity metrics.

CONTEXT & VISION

Muster was conceived during a moment of uncertainty in the tech industry regarding remote work.

While many companies were fully embracing distributed teams, highlighting increased productivity and healthier work-life balance, there were also growing reports of isolation, disconnection, and weakened team relationships.

At the same time, Oak3 was at a mature stage, with a well-structured team and strong technical foundations. We had recently worked on a third-person multiplayer product, gaining valuable experience with shared virtual spaces, presence, and real-time interaction.

With this context in mind, I brought forward the vision for Muster to my partners. I believed it was the right moment to explore a product that could address the social and collaborative gaps of remote work through a gamified, spatial experience.

I presented the core principles of the product and proposed approaching it as a long-term initiative, supported by an investor who could contribute not only financially, but also strategically.

The idea resonated with the team, and we decided to move forward. By building on the technical background and knowledge gained from previous projects, and by securing a strategic partner, we were able to reduce risk while exploring a new product-driven direction.

MY ROLE

On Muster, I worked as Lead Game Designer, Product Owner, and closely collaborated as Producer throughout the project.

This was the first project where I formally acted as a design lead. Instead of working alongside another game designer as peers, I was responsible for supervising the design process, reviewing work, identifying improvements, and guiding design decisions.

Most of the core ideas and solutions originated from my vision, which I then directed another game designer to explore and detail. From there, I reviewed, refined, and challenged those designs to ensure consistency and quality.

In parallel, I was responsible for planning the product from start to finish. This included defining milestones, presenting the roadmap to the investor, and producing regular progress reports.

Whenever the project required a shift in direction or scope, I reassessed the plan, justified the changes, and realigned expectations.

During day-to-day development, I shared production responsibilities with the studio’s producer. Even when I was not acting directly in that role, I remained deeply involved in discussions around prioritization, scope, and decision-making to keep the project aligned with its goals.

DESIGNING A SHARED SPACE

I have worked remotely for many years and across different companies. While working from home brings clear benefits, over time I started to feel the absence of real, spontaneous interaction with colleagues.

Most remote tools reduce people to icons in a list, creating an invisible barrier to conversation and turning the work environment into something cold and rigid.

When I founded Oak3, one of the first alternatives we explored was Gather, a pixel-art virtual space where teams could build a shared office. Simply having an avatar and moving through a virtual environment brought back a sense of physical presence that I had not felt in remote work for a long time.

We used Gather from the very beginning, but over time its limitations became clear. That discomfort led me to research the market and talk with different companies, where I identified recurring pain points related to isolation, lack of presence, and friction in spontaneous communication.

One of the first design decisions for Muster was to create a 3D shared space with a more professional tone, reinforcing that this was a work environment rather than a playful abstraction.

The perspective was intentionally kept consistent, allowing users to always understand where others were and what was happening around them. This spatial legibility helps users decide when and how to interact, without requiring explicit prompts.

Each user has their own desk, and interactions are communicated through simple visual cues, such as avatars gathering together or conversation indicators appearing above them.

These signals were designed to act as implicit rules, teaching users how to behave in the space without tutorials or instructions. Presence, availability, and focus are communicated visually, not verbally.

Additional layers, such as personal status indicators and optional private spaces, were designed to give users control over their level of social exposure.

The broader space was structured to support different social contexts. Meeting rooms, shared areas like a kitchen, and informal spaces encourage movement and create situations similar to real-world office dynamics.

This approach works because it builds on behaviors people already understand from physical offices. Instead of forcing users to learn new interaction patterns, Muster recreates familiar social dynamics in a virtual environment.

SYSTEMS BEHIND THE SPACE

In traditional offices, much of team dynamics is understood through informal signals. Managers and teammates naturally perceive who is isolated, who is overloaded, or who has suddenly changed behavior.

In remote work, most of these signals disappear, leaving teams with little context beyond meetings and task completion.

One of Muster’s core design goals was to recover these signals without introducing invasive or controlling systems.

Instead of focusing on individual productivity, the platform was designed to observe interaction patterns and spatial behavior at a high level, transforming everyday actions into readable signals about team dynamics.

Behind the scenes, Muster aggregates information such as presence, movement, interaction frequency, and shared spaces usage to reveal trends over time.

The intent is not to monitor individuals, but to help teams and leaders understand how collaboration is unfolding and when intervention or support might be needed.

Optional self-reported mood indicators further add context, allowing human conversations to happen earlier and more naturally.

Another key system layer was reducing cognitive overload. Remote work often forces people to juggle multiple tools simultaneously, fragmenting attention and breaking flow.

Muster was designed as a central workspace where essential tools can coexist within the same environment, minimizing context switching and keeping users focused on collaboration rather than navigation.

Finally, performance and lightness were treated as design principles. The platform needed to feel present without becoming heavy or intrusive.

Every system decision was guided by the idea that Muster should support daily work quietly in the background, enhancing awareness and connection without competing for attention.

REFLECTION

Muster was the largest and most ambitious product I have worked on so far. Developed over more than a year, it involved close collaboration with the entire studio and required aligning game design thinking with broader, corporate-scale systems.

Designing a product that borrows from game design principles while remaining accessible to a non-gamer audience was one of the project’s main challenges.

Finding the balance between expressiveness, usability, and simplicity demanded constant iteration and careful decision-making.

Because it was a long and complex project, maintaining team motivation and engagement was also a significant responsibility.

Beyond technical challenges, this required clear direction, realistic planning, and continuous communication to keep everyone aligned with the product’s goals.

Throughout Muster’s development, I stepped fully into the role of Lead Game Designer, guiding design decisions, supporting the team, and ensuring coherence between vision and execution.

Although this role was new to me, it became one of the most valuable learning experiences of my career.

The project successfully reached its planned milestone, delivering a functional MVP now in the hands of the investor.

More importantly, Muster reinforced my ability to lead design at scale, navigate ambiguity, and build complex systems that prioritize human interaction and clarity.
