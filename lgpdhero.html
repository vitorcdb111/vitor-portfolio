LGPD Hero

Released: 2024

Studio: Oak3 Game Studio

Platforms: WebGL

Skill Focus: Game Design, Level Design, UX Design, Production Management

Time on Project: ~6 months

Team size: 7 people

Engine: Unity

WHAT IS THE PROJECT?

LGPD Hero is an educational game designed to teach Brazil’s General Data Protection Law (LGPD) playfully and engagingly, complementing corporate training programs.

Set in the fictional universe of the Galactic Patrol, the game transforms legal principles into interactive experiences. Players learn by exploring, talking to characters, and interacting with the world across different missions.

At its peak, the game reached an average of 500 monthly subscribers, with Clube de Regatas Flamengo as its main client.

SUMMARY OF CONTRIBUTIONS

I was the sole Game Designer responsible for developing all of the game’s core systems. I built the concept, universe, mechanics, AI systems, level design, and the adaptation of legal concepts into game mechanics.

I also managed end-to-end development, created milestones, and validated deliverables with our partners, Evoluto and SecBrain. Each of these areas is explained in detail in the sections below.

PROJECT CONTEXT

The idea for LGPD Hero started after Evoluto identified a major issue with LGPD compliance. Many companies were struggling to help their teams truly understand the law and its principles.

To address this, we partnered with SecBrain, a company specializing in LGPD, to guide us on the key legal aspects while we focused on transforming that knowledge into an engaging interactive experience.

We proposed a game-based training solution, something that could teach employees through play, using both direct and indirect learning, rich analogies, and story-driven interactions to make data protection fun and memorable.

The biggest challenge was designing something that non-gamers could understand and enjoy. It couldn’t feel like a quiz. It needed to be simple, intuitive, and genuinely entertaining while still focused on the law’s core concepts.

That’s when I proposed a third-person shooter, exactly the kind of idea that sticks in someone’s mind, especially an HR manager hearing about a “data protection shooter”.

Of course, I’m not completely crazy. The game is an automatic shooter, inspired by Archero. The player moves freely through the map, and when enemies get close, the system automatically fires. This approach allows players to focus on what really matters: completing missions and learning.

IDEA CONCEPTION

The hardest part of this project was, without a doubt, the law itself. The LGPD is dense and complex, not exactly the kind of material you’d expect to turn into a fun game.

I needed a universe that could help me build analogies, a world that felt engaging and familiar while giving me creative freedom. After thinking for a while, I decided it would be a sci-fi game inspired by Star Wars.

It was the perfect fit. My target audience consists mostly of adults who grew up during the golden age of Star Wars. That connection alone would make the experience instantly nostalgic and approachable.

The premise is:

“The Cyber Galaxy, the final frontier. Guided by the principles of the General Data Protection Law (LGPD), the Galactic Patrol Academy stands as the guardian of the universe. Its mission is to maintain order and enforce the security laws that govern the digital world. These are the journeys of its patrol officers.”

Once I had the world, I began designing analogies between the law and the gameplay. The LGPD contains several key principles, and I wanted each of them to appear naturally as mechanics, items, or characters in the game.

TURNING LGPD PRINCIPLES INTO GAMEPLAY

I mapped several key LGPD concepts directly into the game’s world and systems so players could learn through interaction, not just text.

Data: the star of the LGPD

Data is represented by the Codex, an in-game item available in different colors, each representing a type of data. Every mission in the game revolves around protecting, recovering, or analyzing these Codexes.

The DPO (Data Protection Officer)

In the real world, the DPO teaches, guides, and ensures compliance. In the game, this became the Admiral, leader of the Galactic Patrol, who trains the player and ensures the law is respected across the galaxy.

Enemies

Most cyberattacks are executed by automated programs that break through security systems by testing multiple codes, essentially, bots. In LGPD Hero, these become combat droids, the main representation of hostile actors in the Cyber Galaxy.

Phishing

To teach social engineering, I introduced Hacked Droids, corrupted allies that look almost identical to friendly patrol droids. They visually communicate phishing: something that looks safe but is actually harmful.

The Verify Gun

The player’s main weapon is the Verify Gun, a reference to the processes of data analysis and integrity validation. When used, it interrupts enemy actions and forces them to drop data fragments, reinforcing the concept of protection through active verification.

COMMON DROIDS

The most common enemy in the game, the Droid, was totally inspired by the B1 battle droids from Star Wars. I wanted this enemy to feel easy and almost comical, much like the original B1s.

In the Star Wars universe, B1s are fragile, unintelligent, and often appear in large groups. That combination makes them both funny and harmless, and that’s exactly the feeling I wanted to recreate in LGPD Hero.

Our droids behave the same way: they appear in groups, they’re easy to defeat, and they exist to make the player feel confident and in control. However, while a single droid is weak, a group of them can quickly become overwhelming if the player ignores them.

This balance creates a subtle tension: they’re not individually dangerous, but they demand attention and crowd management. The design intention was clear: this enemy isn’t meant to be threatening. It’s meant to keep the player alert and active, rewarding quick decision-making and spatial awareness.

HACKED DROIDS

If the regular Droid is the simple, comic-relief enemy, the Hacked Droid is its opposite, designed to create moments of tension and act as a kind of mini-boss during gameplay.

This enemy has more health and a more complex behavior pattern. Once it detects the player’s presence, it immediately begins to attack.

Unlike the common Droids, Hacked Droids don’t appear in groups. Instead, they’re strategically placed to surprise the player.

The key point is that their appearance is almost identical to the regular patrol Droids from the Galactic Patrol. This visual similarity forces players to pay attention and identify the enemy before engaging, teaching the concept of phishing indirectly through gameplay.

In terms of combat behavior, the Hacked Droid charges at the player with a tackle attack. After this move, it becomes temporarily disoriented, giving players a brief window to react, reposition, or plan their next action. This design keeps the encounter dynamic, mixing small bursts of panic with short moments of relief and strategy.

SCORE SYSTEM

When the player begins a level, they receive a main mission and a short lore introduction, along with new insights into LGPD principles. After that, the player is free to explore the map at their own pace.

LGPD Hero was designed as a training tool for real DPOs (Data Protection Officers) to help them evaluate how engaged their teams are during the learning process. To support this goal, I created a score and reporting system that tracks not only the player’s success but also how they interact with the educational content.

To reach the victory condition, the player simply needs to complete the main mission. However, throughout the level, players can earn additional points for performing various actions that reinforce LGPD concepts:

  • Defeating enemies: each enemy defeated grants points and drops a data fragment, reinforcing the idea of data protection. Collecting these fragments is optional but contributes to the final score.
  • Recovering dropped items: when the player dies, they drop a bag containing their personal data. Recovering it references another LGPD principle and also rewards the player with points.
  • Exploration and interaction: computers and NPCs across the level provide gameplay tips and information about LGPD. Each interaction grants points, encouraging curiosity and engagement.
  • Completing side quests: finishing all secondary objectives in a mission rewards the player with a special badge and bonus points, recognizing deeper exploration and mastery.

All actions are tracked on the backend and recorded in a final report for the DPO. This allows trainers to see not only who completed the mission, but also how each player behaved, whether they explored, interacted, or simply rushed to the end.

To promote ongoing participation, LGPD Hero features an in-game leaderboard accessible to all players. It displays team rankings and monthly scores, creating friendly competition and motivating employees to replay missions and improve their results.

HUD & UX DESIGN

I worked on the user experience and interface flow for some in-game screens, focusing on the HUD design. My goal was to ensure that information was always clear, intuitive, and well-positioned during gameplay.

Inspired by titles like Lego Star Wars and other 3D platformers, I designed the layout and logic of the HUD, defining where and how each element would appear on screen to improve clarity and maintain immersion.

I focused on flow and spatial logic, making sure players could easily understand their status, objectives, and resources without distraction or confusion.

To clarify, I was responsible for the experience design, screen flow, positioning, and player readability, while the final art and visual polish were created by the art team.

The result was a clean and accessible interface that supported player learning and focus without overwhelming the screen, aligning perfectly with the game’s educational purpose.

PRODUCTION & MANAGEMENT

As Project Manager, I was responsible for planning all milestones and deliverables throughout the project. I regularly reported progress to our partners and clearly explained each development phase to ensure full alignment between the production team and stakeholders.

During development, we used Jira as our main tool for task tracking and monitoring, following the Scrum methodology. We conducted regular planning meetings and daily stand-ups to keep communication active and transparent.

At the start of the project, we worked in one-week sprints, which helped us stay agile and react quickly to changes. As the project and team matured, we shifted to a two-week sprint cycle, allowing for deeper iteration and more structured progress.

We adopted a strict rule: a task was only considered “done” once it had been tested and validated. We defined clear milestones to mark tangible progress and maintain a steady production rhythm.

Given the complexity of LGPD Hero, I planned an early milestone dedicated to a playable short version, which we used to validate the concept with the target audience.

After gathering feedback, I led a team retrospective to reorganize priorities and adjust our development roadmap. This approach allowed us to refine gameplay and improve educational clarity before full production, ensuring the project met both design and training goals.
